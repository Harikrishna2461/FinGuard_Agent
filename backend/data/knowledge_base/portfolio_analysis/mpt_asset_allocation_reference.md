## What Is Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)?

Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) is a mathematical framework used to build a portfolio of assets that maximizes the expected return for the collective level of risk. American economist Harry Markowitz pioneered this theory in his paper "Portfolio Selection," published in the Journal of Finance in 1952. He was later awarded a Nobel Prize for his work on modern portfolio theory.

A key part of MPT is diversification. Most investments are either high risk and high return or low risk and low return. Markowitz argued that investors could achieve their best results by choosing an optimal mix of the two based on an assessment of their individual tolerance to risk.

## Core Principles

### Risk Aversion

MPT assumes that investors are risk-averse, meaning they prefer a less risky portfolio to a riskier one for a given level of return. As a practical matter, risk aversion implies that most people should invest in multiple asset classes.

### Expected Return Calculation

The expected return of a portfolio is calculated as a weighted sum of the returns of the individual assets. Example: if a portfolio contains four equally weighted assets with expected returns of 4%, 6%, 10%, and 14%, the portfolio's expected return would be:

(4% × 25%) + (6% × 25%) + (10% × 25%) + (14% × 25%) = 8.5%

### Portfolio Risk (Variance)

The portfolio's risk is a function of the variances of each asset and the correlations of each pair of assets. Because of asset correlations, the total portfolio risk (standard deviation) is lower than what would be calculated by a weighted sum. This is the mathematical basis for diversification benefits.

### Individual vs. Portfolio Analysis

MPT argues that any given investment's risk and return characteristics should not be viewed alone but should be evaluated by how it affects the overall portfolio's risk and return. An investor can construct a portfolio of multiple assets that will result in greater returns without a higher level of risk.

## The Efficient Frontier

The efficient frontier is a cornerstone of MPT. Every possible combination of assets can be plotted on a graph, with the portfolio's risk on the X-axis and the expected return on the Y-axis. This plot reveals the most desirable combinations.

An upward-sloping curve connecting all of the most efficient portfolios is called the efficient frontier. Investing in a portfolio underneath the curve is not desirable because it does not maximize returns for a given level of risk.

When a portfolio falls to the right of the efficient frontier, it possesses greater risk relative to its predicted return. When it falls beneath the slope, it offers a lower level of return relative to risk.

## Diversification Benefits

### Negative Correlation

Stock investors can reduce risk by adding government bond ETFs. The variance of the portfolio will be significantly lower because government bonds have a negative correlation with stocks. Small-cap value stocks often do well during periods of high inflation when bonds do poorly. As a result, the portfolio's overall volatility can be lower than all-bond portfolios, with higher expected returns.

### Example

Suppose Portfolio A has an expected return of 8.5% and a standard deviation of 8%. Portfolio B has an expected return of 8.5% and a standard deviation of 9.5%. Portfolio A is more efficient because it has the same expected return but lower risk.

## Asset Allocation Strategies

### Strategic Asset Allocation

Long-term allocation based on investor's risk tolerance, time horizon, and financial goals:
- **Conservative**: 20-30% equities, 50-60% bonds, 10-20% cash/alternatives
- **Moderate**: 50-60% equities, 30-40% bonds, 5-10% alternatives
- **Aggressive**: 70-90% equities, 10-20% bonds, 0-10% alternatives

### Tactical Asset Allocation

Short-term deviations from strategic allocation to capitalize on market conditions or economic outlook.

### Rebalancing

Periodically adjusting portfolio weights back to target allocations. Rebalancing disciplines include:
- Calendar-based (quarterly, semi-annually, annually)
- Threshold-based (rebalance when allocation drifts beyond a set percentage band, e.g., ±5%)

## Criticisms of MPT

- Evaluates portfolios based on variance rather than downside risk. Two portfolios with the same variance are equally desirable under MPT even if one has frequent small losses and the other has rare but spectacular declines.
- Most investors would prefer frequent small losses over rare catastrophic declines.
- Post-Modern Portfolio Theory (PMPT) attempts to improve MPT by minimizing downside risk instead of variance.

## Practical Application

Regular investors apply MPT by:
- Assessing their risk tolerance
- Creating a diversified portfolio across multiple asset classes (stocks, bonds, real estate, etc.) with low correlations
- Investing in target-date mutual funds or well-diversified ETFs, which apply MPT principles

## Key Metrics

| Metric | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Sharpe Ratio | Risk-adjusted return: (Return - Risk-free rate) / Standard deviation |
| Beta | Systematic risk relative to the market (beta = 1 means market-level risk) |
| Alpha | Excess return relative to a benchmark after adjusting for risk |
| R-squared | Percentage of portfolio's movements explained by benchmark movements |
| Tracking Error | Standard deviation of the difference between portfolio and benchmark returns |
