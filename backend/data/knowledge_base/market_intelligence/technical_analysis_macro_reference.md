# Technical Analysis and Market Intelligence Reference

> Sources: Investopedia (Technical Analysis, by Adam Hayes, updated Dec 2025; RSI Indicator, by Jason Fernando, updated Dec 2025)

## What Is Technical Analysis?

Technical analysis is a trading method that evaluates trading activity data, focusing on price and volume, to find investment opportunities and determine optimal trade entry and exit points. It contrasts with fundamental analysis, which evaluates a security's value based on financial data such as earnings and sales.

Technical analysis can be applied to any security with historical trading data, including stocks, derivatives, commodities, fixed-income securities, currencies, and forex. It is particularly common in commodities and forex markets where traders focus on short-term price changes.

## Core Principles (Charles Dow Theory)

1. **The market discounts everything** — All information (fundamentals, market psychology, etc.) is already priced into the security. The only remaining analysis is of price movements.

2. **Price moves in trends** — Prices, even in random market movements, exhibit trends regardless of the time frame observed. A stock price is more likely to continue a past trend than to move erratically.

3. **History tends to repeat itself** — The repetitive nature of price movements is attributed to market psychology (emotions of fear and excitement). Chart patterns illustrate emotions and subsequent market movements.

## Key Technical Indicators

### Relative Strength Index (RSI)

The RSI is a momentum oscillator measuring the speed and magnitude of a security's recent price changes. Developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. and introduced in his 1978 book "New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems."

**Calculation (14-period standard)**:
- Step 1: RSI = 100 - [100 / (1 + Average Gain / Average Loss)]
- Step 2 (smoothed): RSI = 100 - [100 / (1 + ((Previous Avg Gain × 13) + Current Gain) / ((Previous Avg Loss × 13) + Current Loss))]

**Key Levels**:
- RSI above 70: Overbought — potential price drop or reversal
- RSI below 30: Oversold — potential price rise or reversal
- RSI at 50: Neutral balance between bullish and bearish

**RSI Divergences**:
- Bullish divergence: Price makes lower low but RSI forms higher low
- Bearish divergence: Price makes higher high but RSI forms lower high

**Swing Rejections** (four-part signals):
- Bullish: RSI falls below 30, crosses back above, dips without going below 30 again, breaks recent high
- Bearish: RSI rises above 70, crosses back below, rallies without reaching 70, breaks recent low

**Limitations**: RSI can stay overbought or oversold for extended periods during strong trends. Most reliable in trading ranges rather than trending markets.

### Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

A trend-following momentum indicator showing the relationship between two moving averages:
- **MACD Line**: 12-period EMA minus 26-period EMA
- **Signal Line**: 9-day EMA of the MACD line
- **Buy signal**: MACD crosses above signal line
- **Sell signal**: MACD crosses below signal line

MACD measures the relationship between two EMAs, while RSI measures price change momentum relative to recent highs and lows. The two indicators are complementary.

### Moving Averages

- **Simple Moving Average (SMA)**: Arithmetic mean of closing prices over n periods
- **Exponential Moving Average (EMA)**: Weighted moving average giving more weight to recent prices
- **Golden Cross**: Short-term MA crosses above long-term MA (bullish)
- **Death Cross**: Short-term MA crosses below long-term MA (bearish)
- Common periods: 20-day, 50-day, 100-day, 200-day

### Bollinger Bands

- Middle band: 20-period SMA
- Upper band: Middle band + 2 standard deviations
- Lower band: Middle band - 2 standard deviations
- Bands widen during high volatility and narrow during low volatility

## Chart Patterns

### Reversal Patterns
- Head and Shoulders (bearish reversal)
- Inverse Head and Shoulders (bullish reversal)
- Double Top (bearish reversal)
- Double Bottom (bullish reversal)

### Continuation Patterns
- Triangles (ascending, descending, symmetrical)
- Flags and Pennants
- Rectangles

### Support and Resistance

Support is a price level where buying interest is strong enough to prevent further decline. Resistance is where selling pressure prevents further advance. Broken support often becomes resistance and vice versa.

## Volume Analysis

Volume confirms price movements:
- Rising price + rising volume = strong uptrend
- Rising price + falling volume = weakening uptrend
- Falling price + rising volume = strong downtrend
- Falling price + falling volume = weakening downtrend

## Macroeconomic Indicators for Market Analysis

| Indicator | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|
| GDP Growth | Overall economic output and health |
| CPI / Inflation | Purchasing power and interest rate expectations |
| Unemployment Rate | Labor market health and consumer spending capacity |
| Federal Funds Rate | Monetary policy direction, borrowing costs |
| PMI (Manufacturing/Services) | Leading indicator of economic activity |
| Consumer Confidence | Forward-looking spending expectations |
| Yield Curve (2Y-10Y spread) | Recession predictor when inverted |
| VIX (Volatility Index) | Market fear/greed gauge ("fear index") |

## Fundamental vs. Technical Analysis

- **Fundamental**: Evaluates intrinsic value through earnings, expenses, assets, liabilities
- **Technical**: Focuses solely on price and volume data, no intrinsic value assessment
- Professional analysts often use both methods in conjunction
- Technical analysis provides entry/exit timing; fundamental analysis identifies what to buy
