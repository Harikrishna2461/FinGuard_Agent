# SEC and FINRA Regulations Reference

> Sources: FINRA.org (Rule 2111 Suitability, Margin Regulation), SEC.gov (Day Trading: Your Dollars at Risk), Investor.gov (Pattern Day Trader)

## FINRA Rule 2111 — Suitability

FINRA Rule 2111 requires that a member or an associated person must have a reasonable basis to believe that a recommended transaction or investment strategy involving a security or securities is suitable for the customer, based on the information obtained through the reasonable diligence of the member or associated person to ascertain the customer's investment profile.

### Three Suitability Obligations

1. **Reasonable-Basis Suitability** — requires a member or associated person to have a reasonable basis to believe, based on reasonable diligence, that the recommendation is suitable for at least some investors. A member or associated person fulfills this obligation by performing reasonable diligence to understand the potential risks and rewards associated with a recommended security or strategy.

2. **Customer-Specific Suitability** — requires that a member or associated person have a reasonable basis to believe that the recommendation is suitable for a particular customer based on that customer's investment profile. Among the factors considered are the customer's age, other investments, financial situation and needs, tax status, investment objectives, investment experience, investment time horizon, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, and any other information the customer may disclose.

3. **Quantitative Suitability** — requires a member or associated person who has actual or de facto control over a customer account to have a reasonable basis for believing that a series of recommended transactions, even if suitable when viewed in isolation, are not excessive and unsuitable for the customer when taken together in light of the customer's investment profile.

### Customer Investment Profile Factors

A customer's investment profile includes, but is not limited to, the customer's age, other investments, financial situation and needs, tax status, investment objectives, investment experience, investment time horizon, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance.

### Institutional Customer Exemption

A member or associated person fulfills the customer-specific suitability obligation for an institutional account if the member or associated person has a reasonable basis to believe that the institutional customer is capable of evaluating investment risks independently, both in general and with regard to particular transactions and investment strategies, and the institutional customer affirmatively indicates that it is exercising independent judgment.

### Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI)

Effective June 30, 2020, the SEC adopted Regulation Best Interest (Rule 15l-1 under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934). A broker-dealer must act in the best interest of a retail customer when making a recommendation without placing its own financial or other interest ahead of the customer's interest.

## FINRA Margin Regulation

### Regulation T (Federal Reserve Board)

Under Reg T, the initial margin requirement for equity securities is 50% of the purchase price — meaning a broker-dealer can lend a customer up to 50% of the purchase price for eligible equity securities.

### FINRA Rule 4210 — Margin Requirements

Sets forth both initial and maintenance margin requirements for positions carried by FINRA members. Maintenance margin is the minimum equity that must be maintained in a margin account on an ongoing basis.

### FINRA Rule 4220 — Daily Record of Required Margin

Requires each member carrying margin accounts to prepare and maintain a daily record of the required margin on a security-by-security basis.

### Portfolio Margin Methodology

Portfolio margin is a risk-based margining methodology that sets margin requirements using a model that calculates the theoretical maximum potential loss of all positions using multiple pricing scenarios.

### Margin Disclosure Requirements

Rule 2264 requires members to deliver a margin disclosure statement describing the risks of margin trading. Rule 4210(g) sets forth specific disclosure and approval requirements for portfolio margin accounts.

## SEC Day Trading Rules

### Day Trading: Your Dollars at Risk (SEC Publication)

Key risks identified by the SEC:
- Day traders typically suffer severe financial losses in their first months of trading, and many never graduate to profit-making status
- Day trading is an extremely stressful and expensive full-time job
- Day traders depend heavily on borrowing money or buying stocks on margin
- Don't believe claims of easy profits or "hot tips"

### Pattern Day Trader Rule (FINRA/SEC)

A pattern day trader is any customer who executes four or more day trades within five business days, provided that the number of day trades represents more than 6% of the customer's total trades in the margin account for that same five business day period.

Requirements:
- Must maintain minimum equity of $25,000 in their margin account on any day that they day trade
- The required minimum equity must be in the account prior to any day-trading activities
- Pattern day trading can only be conducted in a margin account
