# AML, KYC, and FATF Guidelines Reference

> Sources: FinCEN.gov (Bank Secrecy Act, Filing Information), OFAC/Treasury.gov (Sanctions FAQs), FATF-GAFI.org (40 Recommendations), Investopedia (KYC Requirements)

## Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) — FinCEN

The Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), also known as the Currency and Foreign Transactions Reporting Act of 1970, requires financial institutions to assist government agencies in detecting and preventing money laundering. The BSA authorizes the U.S. Department of the Treasury to impose requirements on financial institutions to keep records of financial transactions and file reports.

### Key BSA Requirements

- Keep records of cash purchases of negotiable instruments
- File Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) for cash transactions exceeding $10,000 daily aggregate amount
- Report suspicious activity via Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)

### BSA Legal Framework

Codified at 12 U.S.C. §§ 1829b, 1951–1960 and 31 U.S.C. §§ 5311–5336. Implementing regulations at 31 CFR Chapter X.

### BSA Filing Forms (via BSA E-Filing System)

| Form | Number | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| Suspicious Activity Report | SAR (Form 111) | Report suspicious transactions |

## Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)

A SAR is a confidential compliance document filed under the BSA to alert FinCEN about potentially illegal activity. SARs must be filed within 30 calendar days from the date of initial detection. Disclosure to the customer is prohibited. SARs must be retained for five years.

### Common Suspicious Activity Patterns (FinCEN)

- Lack of evidence of legitimate business activity by the parties
- Unusual financial nexuses among certain business types (e.g., food importer dealing with auto parts exporter)
- Transactions not commensurate with the stated business type
- Unusually large numbers/volumes of wire transfers or repetitive patterns
- Unusually complex series of transactions involving multiple accounts, banks, and parties
- Bulk cash and monetary instrument transactions
- Unusual mixed deposits into a business account
- Bursts of transactions within short periods, especially in dormant accounts
- Transactions inconsistent with the expected purpose of the account
- Transactions attempting to avoid reporting and recordkeeping requirements (structuring)

## OFAC Sanctions Compliance

### What OFAC Does

OFAC administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals against targeted foreign countries and regimes, terrorists, international narcotics traffickers, and those engaged in proliferation of weapons of mass destruction.

### Who Must Comply

All U.S. persons must comply, including: all U.S. citizens and permanent resident aliens regardless of location, all persons and entities within the United States, and all U.S. incorporated entities and their foreign branches.

### SDN List and the 50 Percent Rule

Any entity owned 50% or more, directly or indirectly, by one or more blocked persons is itself considered blocked. Blocked property must be reported to OFAC within 10 business days.

### Penalties

Civil penalties can reach millions of dollars per violation. Criminal penalties include up to $20 million in fines and 30 years imprisonment per willful violation.

## Know Your Customer (KYC) Requirements

### Three Components (per Investopedia/FINRA/FinCEN)

1. **Customer Identification Program (CIP)** — Obtain name, date of birth, address, and identification number
2. **Customer Due Diligence (CDD)** — Gather credentials to confirm identity and assess risk for suspicious activities
3. **Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)** — Additional collection for high-risk customers (terrorism financing, money laundering risk)

### FINRA KYC Rules

- **FINRA Rule 2090** — Requires reasonable diligence when opening and maintaining client accounts
- **FINRA Rule 2111** — Requires reasonable basis to believe a recommendation is suitable based on client's financial situation

## FATF 40 Recommendations

The FATF Recommendations are internationally endorsed global standards covering seven areas:

1. AML/CFT Policies and Coordination
2. Money Laundering and Confiscation
3. Terrorist Financing and Financing of Proliferation
4. Preventive Measures
5. Transparency and Beneficial Ownership
6. Powers and Responsibilities of Competent Authorities
7. International Cooperation

The cornerstone is the risk-based approach: countries and institutions must identify, assess, and understand their ML/TF risks and take commensurate measures. The FATF assesses effectiveness against 11 immediate outcomes.
